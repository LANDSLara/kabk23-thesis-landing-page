Graphic Design Theses, KABK, 2022/23
▮ Ro Antia • On Slowing Down, a Thought Index • How can obsolete techniques like pinhole photography be used as a method in working towards a slower, simpler way to experience time? ▮ Andon Bakarov • Inside A Hibernation Den • This thesis examines the leftover traces of captivity inone traumatic relationship between two species with questionable hierarchies. The practice of taming bears and forcing them into ‘dancing’for entertainment has beena tradition of Roma communities since centuries on and outside of the Balkans. ▮ Lara Dautun • Two Stories of Feminist Publishing • This Thesis presents a state of research, that will probably never be final, about independent feminist publishing in the Netherlands during Second-wave feminism. ▮ Lulu van Dijck • Have You Considered A Garden? • In this text, I offer some notes on gender, dualism, ecology, the garden, the digital and the glitch, in attempt to give body to a non–linear understanding of the philosophical roots of oppressive structures in what we essentially call the global North,and the places its colonialist tentacles have reached. ▮ Marieke Dijsselhof • THE MYTHS AND THE FAITHFULLY PLASTIC HORSES • My relationship with toys has always been something mystic. Dreams, imagination, play, and magic. The combination of the unconscious, conscious, and uncertainty glide us into a world where fantasy becomes reality. ▮ Elina Ersdorf • Digital: Entropy vs Polution • This thesis explores the complex relationship between nature and technology, specifically focusing on the issue of data management in the face of exponential growth and entropic forces. ▮ Ronaldo Wold Figueiredo • Coloring Outside the Lines • The thesis seeks to demonstrate the importance of maintaining free and unstructured spaces in the city for fostering innovation, diversity, and community interaction. What can we do? ▮ Einar Vidar Gudmundsson • The Armchair Sailor and the Beach Leopard • With this thesis, I am searching for questions regarding mourning, a sort of posthumous bonding, and hopefully a decent adventure. ▮ Jannie Guo • Deformation of Time • My thesis circulates between productivity, time, and relational autonomy. A part is dedicated to an experiment of a ‘silent retreat’ of 24 hours. ▮ Patrick Hutchinson • The Precise Moment a Copy Becomes Independent • This thesis aims to evaluate current laws of copyright infringements in trying to establish when and how a copy may become distinct from its original. Ideas of adaptation and modification are established and, drawn in parallel to open-source culture, aim to question the state of established copyright rulings online. ▮ Isabella Elida Inan-Carter • THE KİLİM:A disappearing art, a hidden language, the work, words, and ways of women... • Turkish carpet weaving is slowly disappearing. In the past, carpets would be woven in almost every household but now only few continue this tradition. In this essay I will explore the changing nature of traditional Aegean village life, particularly for that of women. ▮ Valters Kalsers • Stalins Revenge • Heracleum sosnovskyi, commonly known as Sosnovky Hogweed or Giant Hogweed, is a plant that is native to the Caucasus region but has become a common weed in many post-Soviet countries. Infamous for its phototoxic properties and invasive character, it poses a significant threat to both wildlife and humans. ▮ Yeeun Kim • Shaping Back • Chairs have appeared in human history for a long time, but they were not for everyone. Until the Middle Ages, the chair belonged to a ruler or an authority, and everyone could sit in a comfortable chair with a backrest after the Industrial Revolution when mass production became possible. We can find chairs everywhere we feel the irresistible urge to sit down. ▮ Tinka Kovacic • The Rite of Immortality • This project derived from my personal fascination with creativity, the imaginary, death, and memory and how they are interconnected. I am interested in the human need to depict, remember and the need to feel remembered. ▮ Alessandro Lucarini • Childhood lunches are the roots of Ambient Music • With our camcorders and mobile phones, we often prefer to capture moments of everyday life that we want to remember, usually good memories. ▮ Amber Meekel • The Memorial Herbarium • Nature has appealed to the imagination for centuries and has been a great source of inspiration for humanity. Because of its cyclical and transformative aspect, ornaments of plants symbolize immortality. ▮ Hilal Mutluel • Türkiye’s Alphabet Reform: Reimagining the Art and Meaning of Calligraphy and Printing • In 1929, Mustafa Kemal Atatürk, the first president of the newly established Republic of Turkiye, implemented the Alphabet reform. The reform aimed to modernize the Turkish language and promote literacy by replacing the Arabic-based Ottoman script with the Latin script. ▮ Chiara Nowak • Fesmist-tech Design • The hormonal contraceptive pill was introduced as a means to control and regulate the reproductive nature of women. It quickly became a symbol for empowerment and sexual liberation. However, an alternative reality behind the use and prescription of the pill, beyond its initial intention as contraception, has transformed the pill into a method of control-backtracking on its initial promises. ▮ Julia Olijkan • The malleability of man • Our body is a canvas that we use to express our feelings, roots, experieces, beliefs. The way in which is strongly determined by culture and religion.In recent years, a new postmodern revival of extreme body modification has emerged. ▮ Isabel Pereira • When I bring Salt to the Table • How can salt within its conservational qualities within food preparation be used to connect and preserve the ties to our roots? ▮ Maria Rivero Gonzalez • I Can See Things Crumbling • The process of decay is a universal phenomenon that affects every living thing, from the smallest microbe to the largest organism. It is an inescapable part of the natural cycle of life. As humans, we are closely familiar with this process, as we witness it not only in our own lives but also in the world around us. ▮ Samuel Salminen • Toponymocide • Toponyms — also known as place names or geographical names — play a vital role in constructing historical memories, identifying the locations that are absorbed by those memories and granting them a tangible presence. They are a cultural element of shared public capital and spatial infrastructure of our everyday life. This study will, however, argue that toponyms are not mere spatial signifiers; they hold a semantic depth profoundly rooted in power relations and struggles over land, resources and identities. ▮ Simonida Savic • A Vertical Geography of Mushrooms • Magic mushrooms, tripping, shrooms, truffles, blue meanies, mushies, are all terms that we might understand in (western) society as synonyms for the psychedelic drug psilocybin. ▮ Luna van Schadewijk • Revisiting Jamaica’s Train Tracks • In many ways, the past is involved in the present–so much so that they might even be considered indistinguishable. I am particularly interested in spatial markers through which the past enters the present; thearchitectural layout of cities and makeup of buildings, the lines we callborders, statues ofancestors we might ratherforget, plants that crossed continents and obsolete train tracks. ▮ Erae Shin • Ambivalent Feeding • Feeding others generally is considered one of the altruistic behaviours found among living creatures as altruistic behaviour entails providing valuable benefits to others at a personal cost. Handing nutritious food to needy strangers, even if one desires the food, is a uniquely human form of altruistic behaviour. ▮ Renata Sidorenko • The Allure of the Lover Archetype.An Exploration of its Use and Meaning in Advertising, Art, and AI. • This thesisexplores the application of archetypes in advertising and visual art by drawing upon the work of Carl Jung “The Archetypes and the Collective Unconscious”and Margaret Mark and Carol S. Pearson’s book “The Hero and the Outlaw”. ▮ Basia Strzezek • Hobgoblin’s Pleasure • The last decades brought us manifold critiques aiming at the reclamation of the ornament, many of them, such as those of Naomi Schor, are tainted by and considered through the lens of the modernist bias or rather a specific narrative within the modernist approach. ▮ Alexandra Superson • A very short history of food and its representations • This thesis explores how we as individuals but also as a society have over the last centuries slowly but gradually become disconnected from real nourishment due to how food has been communicated to us. ▮ Jacky Tang • Allegories in the Face Saloon • An experimental document that combines prose with short form fiction, to research the human face as a communication tool in the context of linguistics, the myth and the spectacle, dramaturgy, Chinese theatre, face reading and control. Having touched upon all these topics, we’re left with humanity and storytelling. ▮ Alice Vink • The Most Underrated Actor • I feel that as humans we have lost touch with nature, which results in the current problems with biodiversity loss,pollution and exhaustion of the soil. I want to understand better why this is and how we might perhaps regain some of that connection we lost and start living more sustainably. ▮ Leona Wehrenberg • Beyond Entertainment: The Potential of Emotional Design in Video Games • The gaming industry has grown tremendously over the last few years and it doesn’t seem like it’s going to stop any time soon. Games are increasingly becoming a larger part of many lives. ▮ Robin Wielink • Shades of Indigo • This thesis explores how Amsterdam became a leading denim capital and examines color (with a focus on indigo), from its commercial applications to its use as a form of independent expression. ▮ Aiym Zhaishylyk • Bureaucracy: An avant-garde project • This paper will examine bureaucracy together with the avant-garde art of the 20th century.
